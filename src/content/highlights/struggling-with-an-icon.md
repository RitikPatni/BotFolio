# Struggling With an Icon...

## Metadata

## Highlights
- Struggling with an icon (as one does). The struggle here is that the check mark is common for "ok/ready/done". In Sweden it has historically been used to mark wrong answers on tests. The opposite, really. So now I need more understanding on how big of a conflict this really is. 
  ![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/E_Ow54yXIAsHYDy.jpg) ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1437687684508659713))
- Not looking for help Twitter guidance, just sharing a struggle that has roots in cultural differences where a small country is also heavily influenced by the culture of a bigger country with differing mental models.
  Reminds me of my dissertation on translation in fiction. ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1437687686567968773))
- Rabbit hole. Here we go:
  In Sweden, correct answers were traditionally marked with an "R" for "Rätt".
  In Finland, ✓ often stands for väärin, i.e., "wrong", due to its similarity to a slanted v. ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1437695194057609217))
- In Japan, the O mark is used instead of the check mark, and the X or ✓ mark are commonly used for wrong.
  Also known as Marujirushi (丸印) in Japan and Gongpyo (공표(공標), ball mark) in Korea, the symbols "◯" or "⭕" are used to represent affirmation in East Asia. ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1437695195383009280))
- In the Netherlands a 'V' is used to show that things are missing while the flourish of approval (or krul) is used for approving a section or sum.
  Today I learned about "the flourish of approval"! 
  ![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/E_O3xWyX0AIftL3.png) ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1437695200428761090))
- The plot thickens. There is a symbol called "dele" or "deleatur". It is eerily similar to the Dutch "krul" (flourish of approval).
  But, the "dele" – as the name implies – means that something is marked for deletion. So actually not approved.
  https://t.co/ad60rmOXdV 
  ![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/E_P9oG4X0AUhlqj.png) ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1437771639404572672))
- Here's a photo showing the Dutch "krul", or "goedkeuringskrul" as the long name appears to be, being used to grade student work. For questions 9 and 10 you can see how a check mark is used to indicate a wrong answer.
  https://t.co/2hm4PnMTPW 
  ![](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/E_P_L8cWEAc2SHO.jpg) ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1437773408624906248))
- A picture of a graded Swedish assignment would look extremely similar.
  The difference is that the "krul" would be a cursive "R". Which also is not far from how the krul looks, as has been pointed out by others. The check mark for wrong answers would be exactly the same. ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1437819066417307648))
- If you speak Swedish, "godkeuringskrul" could be translated as "godkännandekrull" or "godkännandekringla". An approval 'curl' or 'squiggle'. ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1437820038120189953))
- This piqued some interest so I’ll summarize where I am right now. The icon I was working on is related to healtchare and at-home-testing kits for covid 19. An icon would follow between physical and digital objects to indicate to the person they’re in the right place. ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1438033733253226496))
- Because of constraints it necessarily has to be small. There is a proposed icon for the testing kit itself. To indicate that it is ready for pickup, the idea was to add a checkmark. ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1438033734591295490))
- I would not have worried much about this had I not been aware that a significant number of users will be elderly and internet novices. The people who, I hypothesize, are most likely to associate the check mark with ”wrong”. ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1438033735971131393))
- Given this risk, and the fact that there is not enough time to do reliable research, I am wary of adding the check mark. An alternative may be the letters ”OK”. ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1438033737669922820))
- As this will be used on printed instructions accompanying each testing kit, it’s not an easy change post-decision.
  But this insight provided by @alundbergh actually proved really helpful. A checkmark in a box, rather than standalone, carries more meaning. https://t.co/3p4ct9b6wP ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1438033739402170369))
- In short, I am not ready to finalize the icon. I have a few more days. I will try and remember to follow up here. :) But learning more about right/wrong indicators in different cultures will definitely come in useful in many situations ahead. ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1438033741105045504))
- Love how people are sharing their culture-specific symbols in this thread: https://t.co/5b9MobNQdB ([View Tweet](https://twitter.com/axbom/status/1438392751989706762))
